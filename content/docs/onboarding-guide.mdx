# Onboarding Guide

This guide walks the end-to-end onboarding flow for a new Alliance member,
from initial diligence through general availability on the cross-member
routing layer. The flow is uniform across member categories (substrate
contributors, regulated banks / brokers / TAs, technology partners,
white-label customers) with category-specific variations called out where
relevant.

The complete onboarding asset library lives under
`lux-private/legal/partnerships/template/` (the partnership-template SPA,
the integration brief, the IAM tenant manifest, the KMS root-key
ceremony, the brand-config bootstrap, the CI/CD wiring) and
`lux-private/legal/SOC2/` (the compliance complementary-controls package
that the member completes at provisioning).

## Phase 0 — Diligence and partnership-template SPA

Before any technical provisioning, the prospective member runs through
the diligence and partnership-template flow.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Initial intake at [w3a.foundation/join](https://w3a.foundation/join).**
The prospective member submits the standard intake form (entity
identification, primary jurisdiction(s), regulatory standing, cap-table
summary, technical posture, commercial fit summary).
</Step>

<Step>
**Diligence kickoff.** The coordination layer's onboarding team confirms
receipt within 5 business days and either advances to diligence or returns
with a structured "not at this time" with feedback. Diligence covers:
regulatory standing in the primary jurisdiction(s), cap-table cleanliness
and beneficial-ownership disclosure, existing IP estate and any
conflicting license obligations, technical posture and clean-room
readiness, compliance program maturity, commercial fit.
</Step>

<Step>
**Standard Strategic Partnership Agreement (SPA) template execution.**
The template is `lux-private/legal/partnerships/template/SPA.md`. The
operative commercial terms are the canonical **50/50-above-documented-
costs** template; bespoke terms (deeper revenue share, capacity
commitments, custom IP carve-outs) are negotiated on top of the
boilerplate.
</Step>

<Step>
**Mutual NDA execution** (if not already in place) covering the
diligence + onboarding flow. The standard mutual-NDA is two-year-rolling
auto-renew with perpetual confidentiality on ceremonial materials.
</Step>

<Step>
**License execution** — LEL v1.2 (Tier-2), and SCLA(s) for any Tier-3
LRL-PR modules the member is taking. If the member is taking Hanzo-source
modules, parallel Hanzo license execution.
</Step>

</Steps>

## Phase 1 — Integration brief

With the SPA and licenses in place, the member and the coordination
layer's integration team produce the **integration brief** — the canonical
technical scoping document for the member's product surface.

The integration brief covers:

- **Product surface** — what the member is building / running on the
  Alliance substrate (custody / matching / settlement / RWA tokenization
  / payment processing / agent-economy services / etc.)
- **Substrate modules consumed** — the Tier-1/2/3 modules the member's
  product uses, mapped to the executed license(s)
- **Tenant model** — how the member's customers are isolated (per-
  customer tenant, per-org tenant, per-jurisdiction tenant)
- **Data residency** — where the member's customer data must reside per
  the jurisdictional compliance overlay
- **Routing-layer integration** — what cross-member traffic the member
  receives and emits, with the per-flow SLA expectations
- **Compliance integration** — the member's SOC 2 sub-service organisation
  posture, KYC/AML stack, sanctions-screening stack, AML-monitoring stack
- **Brand integration** — the member's brand assets, the brand-via-env-
  override config, the brand-registry entry
- **CI/CD posture** — the member's GitHub org, container registry,
  release process, environment matrix (dev / staging / prod)
- **Launch sequence** — the soft-launch and GA milestones, with the
  metric criteria for each

The integration brief is the operating contract between the member and
the coordination layer's integration team for the duration of the
onboarding.

## Phase 2 — IAM tenant provisioning

Hanzo IAM is the authoritative identity layer across the Alliance. The
member's tenant provisioning runs in a documented sequence:

<Steps>

<Step>
**Tenant request** — the member submits a tenant request through
`iam.w3a.foundation` (or via the integration team's manifest in
`lux-private/legal/partnerships/template/iam-tenant.yaml`).
</Step>

<Step>
**Tenant seeded** — the integration team provisions the IAM tenant with
the standard `<org>-<app>` naming convention (e.g., `acme-exchange`,
`acme-custody`, `acme-rwa`). Each application is registered separately
under the tenant.
</Step>

<Step>
**Superuser seed** — the member's primary superuser is provisioned with
`z@<member-domain>` (or the member's preferred identity) and the canonical
Alliance superuser-password convention. The superuser sets up
multi-factor at first login.
</Step>

<Step>
**OIDC + JWT integration** — the member's product wires up Hanzo IAM as
the OIDC provider. JWT `owner` claim carries the org identifier that the
member's data layer uses for tenant scoping.
</Step>

<Step>
**Gateway registration** — the member's API surface is registered with
the Alliance gateway (`api.w3a.foundation`) which injects identity headers
into the upstream member request.
</Step>

</Steps>

## Phase 3 — KMS root-key seal

The member's KMS root-key is sealed under the Alliance's KMS root, with
the per-org KEK envelope established for every encrypted data store the
member operates.

<Steps>

<Step>
**KMS tenant provisioned** — the member's KMS tenant is provisioned in
`kms.w3a.foundation`. The tenant carries its own HSM-bound root and is
isolated from every other tenant cryptographically.
</Step>

<Step>
**Org KEK initialised** — for each org the member onboards, an Org KEK is
provisioned in the member's KMS tenant. The Org KEK is the wrapping key
for the per-file / per-row CEKs the member's data layer will use.
</Step>

<Step>
**CEK envelope spec** — the member's data layer implements the standard
envelope: per-file/per-row random AES-256-GCM CEK, encrypted under the
Org KEK, with `key_version` for rotation and `aead_aad` for binding the
ciphertext to its row identity. The envelope spec lives in the canonical
Alliance docs at `kms.w3a.foundation/docs`.
</Step>

<Step>
**Audit-log integration** — every CEK unwrap call is logged to the
member's KMS audit trail, which the SOC 2 sub-service organisation
assessment audits annually.
</Step>

<Step>
**Crypto-shred drill** — the member runs a crypto-shred drill against a
test org before going live. Revoking the Org KEK renders the test org's
entire dataset unreadable, proving the disaster-recovery primitive.
</Step>

</Steps>

## Phase 4 — Brand-config bootstrap

The Alliance enforces **brand-via-env-override discipline**. The member's
brand identity lives in env-overridable config, not in product code, so
substrate upgrades flow cleanly across the network.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Brand config scaffolded** — the member's repo is scaffolded with the
canonical `brand.ts` (or `brand.json` / `brand.toml`) carrying the
minimum interface: `name`, `wordmark`, `domain`, `supportEmail`,
`parentOrg`, plus member-specific extensions.
</Step>

<Step>
**Default config = member's brand.** The repo's default config carries
the member's brand strings. Alternate configs (white-label downstream
partners, jurisdiction-specific overlays) override via env vars at build
time.
</Step>

<Step>
**No hardcoded brand strings in product code.** The integration team's
review pass searches the repo for any hard-coded brand string outside
`brand.ts` and flags them for migration before the soft launch.
</Step>

<Step>
**Brand-registry entry** — the member's brand integration is registered
in the Alliance brand registry. The registry is the canonical reference
for the cross-routing layer when surfacing member branding to upstream
customers.
</Step>

</Steps>

## Phase 5 — CI/CD wiring

The member's CI/CD pipeline integrates with the Alliance build and deploy
substrate.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Repo creation** — the member's repo lives under their own GitHub org
(`github.com/<member>`). The Alliance does not require the repo to live
under a coordination-layer org.
</Step>

<Step>
**Container registry** — the member's container images push to their own
registry (`ghcr.io/<member>` for GitHub-native members, member-specific
registries for sovereign deploys). The Alliance does not require a
shared registry.
</Step>

<Step>
**Multi-arch builds** — the member's build pipeline produces `amd64` and
`arm64` images in parallel using native runners (no QEMU). The
coordination layer's CI/CD team can provision build runners on the
Alliance's existing infrastructure on request.
</Step>

<Step>
**Deployment manifests** — the member's k8s manifests follow the
Alliance Kubernetes conventions: `hanzoai/ingress` and `hanzoai/static`
plugin (no nginx, no caddy), `compose.yml` (not `docker-compose.yml`)
for local development. The shared kustomize base is published at
`lux-private/k8s/base/`.
</Step>

<Step>
**Environment matrix** — dev / staging / prod environment provisioning,
with secrets pulled from KMS (never hardcoded). Each environment has its
own IAM tenant slug, KMS tenant, and brand-config namespace.
</Step>

</Steps>

## Phase 6 — Soft launch

Soft launch is the first live cross-member-routing-layer integration with
limited customer-facing exposure.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Soft-launch readiness review** — the coordination layer's integration
team and the member's engineering lead jointly review the soft-launch
checklist: substrate integration green, IAM live, KMS sealed, brand
registry live, CI/CD pipeline producing builds, SOC 2 complementary
controls in place, runbook published, on-call rota established.
</Step>

<Step>
**Soft-launch traffic** — the coordination layer routes a defined,
limited slice of cross-member traffic to the member's surface. The slice
is calibrated to validate the integration without exposing the member to
operational burst it cannot absorb.
</Step>

<Step>
**Metric review at 30 days** — the integration team and the member review
the soft-launch metrics: latency, error rate, customer-experience
metrics, compliance-event rate, support-load metrics. The review either
advances to GA or returns to engineering for remediation.
</Step>

</Steps>

## Phase 7 — GA

General availability is the full routing-layer integration with the
member's surface available to every Alliance customer in the member's
jurisdictional footprint.

<Steps>

<Step>
**GA announcement** — the coordination layer publishes the GA
announcement through the Alliance comms surface. Member is now a
first-class entry in the member registry, the cross-member routing layer,
and the partner ecosystem.
</Step>

<Step>
**Quarterly coordination forum participation** — the member begins
quarterly forum reporting at the next cycle (financial, operational,
compliance, roadmap).
</Step>

<Step>
**Annual SOC 2 re-attestation** — the member completes the SOC 2
sub-service organisation re-attestation on the standard annual cadence.
</Step>

<Step>
**Continuous coordination** — the member's integration with the substrate
upgrades automatically as substrate releases ship, subject to the
member's release cadence and the brand-via-env-override discipline. New
modules become available under the member's existing LEL / SCLA
without re-execution.
</Step>

</Steps>
