# Governance

The Alliance is a network of independent businesses. The coordination layer
exists not to manage them but to (i) maintain the substrate, (ii) license
the IP, (iii) allocate the capital, (iv) admit new members, and (v) resolve
disputes between members. **The discipline is to keep coordination narrow
and leave operations to members.**

## The coordination layer

The coordination layer sits at **W3A Foundation** (the Delaware C-corp
parent) with **Hanzo, Inc.** as the parallel licensor on the AI side. Its
board includes representatives of the substrate-developer team, the
founding investors, and an independent director slate.

### What the coordination layer holds

- All Tier-2 (LEL) and Tier-3 (LRL-PR) licensing authority
- All patent ownership across the bifurcated W3A Foundation + Hanzo, Inc.
  estate (87 catalogued inventions)
- All trade-secret records
- The Alliance brand registry and brand-policy enforcement
- The Alliance treasury (Layer-1 capital)
- The substrate engineering team
- The shared compliance posture (clean-room protocol, SOC 2 sub-service
  organisation responsibility, vendor management, secops)

### What the coordination layer does **not** hold

- Member operations
- Member-level customer relationships
- Member-level product roadmaps
- Member-level hiring decisions

This separation is load-bearing for the federation model. Members preserve
operational sovereignty; the coordination layer preserves substrate, IP,
brand, capital, and compliance discipline. Anything beyond those five
buckets stays with the member.

## Founder and independent director slate

The W3A Foundation board includes:

- **Founders and substrate-team representatives** holding the technical
  and IP continuity of the Alliance.
- **Founding investors** with multi-cycle horizons, sized to provide
  capital-deployment continuity across the five-to-seven-year compounding
  window.
- **Independent directors** drawn from the regulated-financial-services,
  cryptography, and federal-mission communities. The independent slate
  provides the legitimacy, oversight discipline, and diligence-grade
  posture that the licensed members require to operate under the
  Alliance umbrella.

The independent director count is calibrated to remain a non-trivial
fraction of the board at every stage; the substrate-team and investor
seats do not control the board unilaterally. The exact composition is
disclosed in the Alliance's annual governance report and is reviewed
under the SOC 2 sub-service organisation assessment.

## Member-coordination forum

The member-coordination forum is a **quarterly information-sharing forum**,
not a decision-making body. Member participation is mandatory for every
admitted member; the agenda is published two weeks in advance and rotates
across:

- Substrate roadmap and engineering updates
- Cross-member distribution accounting (50/50-above-documented-costs
  routing-layer reconciliation)
- Capital pool deployment and member-stabilisation discussions where
  relevant
- Compliance posture updates across operating jurisdictions
- New-member admission previews
- Pending Phase-Three transactions where members have economic interest

Decisions that require member input — substrate-roadmap changes that
affect member integration, new-member admissions in jurisdictions where
member overlap matters, capital allocations that draw on Layer-1 reserves
— run through documented written consultation outside the forum. The
forum is the venue for visibility; written process is the venue for
decisions.

## When the coordination layer intervenes in member operations

The coordination layer intervenes in member operations only under
narrowly-defined conditions:

1. **The member is in material breach of its license terms.** The breach
   is documented, time-bounded, and subject to cure under the
   underlying LEL / SCLA. Intervention is licensing-side, not
   operational.
2. **The member is in material breach of the clean-room protocol.**
   Derived IP that should belong to W3A / Hanzo has been mis-allocated;
   the intervention restores the correct allocation under the
   clean-room policy.
3. **The member's compliance posture has deteriorated** to the point of
   creating regulatory risk to other members in the same jurisdiction.
   The intervention is the minimum action needed to ring-fence the
   contagion (commonly a temporary suspension of cross-routing into the
   affected member's surface until the compliance posture is remediated).
4. **The member is failing financially and has drawn member-stabilisation
   distribution** from the Alliance treasury. The coordination layer
   attaches operational conditions to that distribution (typical
   conditions: monthly financial reporting, hiring freeze, capital
   spend cap, board observer seat) until the member is back to standalone
   viability or to an orderly wind-down.
5. **A material dispute between two or more members** requires
   coordination-layer adjudication. The dispute-resolution mechanism
   below is the operative process.

In each case the intervention is documented, time-bounded, and subject to
the dispute-resolution mechanism in the underlying Strategic Partnership
Agreement.

## Dispute resolution

The standard Strategic Partnership Agreement carries a three-stage
dispute-resolution mechanism:

<Steps>

<Step>
**Operational dispute** — direct negotiation between the principals of the
disputing members, with a 30-day window. Most disputes are resolved at
this stage; the cost of escalation is high relative to the typical
operational disagreement.
</Step>

<Step>
**Coordination-layer mediation** — if direct negotiation fails, the
dispute escalates to the coordination layer's general counsel, who
mediates under the documented mediation protocol. The mediation has a
60-day window and a non-binding recommendation as its output. If both
members accept the recommendation, the dispute is resolved; if either
member rejects, the dispute proceeds to binding arbitration.
</Step>

<Step>
**Binding arbitration** — under the governing-law clause of the Strategic
Partnership Agreement (typically Delaware for US members, English law for
UK / Crown Dependencies, Luxembourg for EU, Singapore for the SE Asia
corridor), administered by the arbitration forum specified in the SPA
(typically AAA / ICC). Arbitration outcomes are binding and enforceable;
the Alliance's policy is that arbitration is the path of last resort and
that members are encouraged to resolve at stages 1 or 2 whenever possible.
</Step>

</Steps>

The dispute-resolution mechanism is **scoped to inter-member disputes
within the Alliance perimeter**. Disputes between a member and an external
counterparty (a customer, a vendor, a regulator) are the member's own
business and run through the member's own dispute-resolution channels.
The coordination layer is not a substitute for the member's own legal,
compliance, or operational posture in the outside world.

## Exit mechanics

The Alliance contemplates exits at three levels:

| Exit level | Mechanism | What persists |
|---|---|---|
| **Individual member exit** | Member raises a strategic round or executes a strategic sale | Alliance retains documented IP-licensing revenue post-exit; member founders take member-level liquidity; cap-table flow proceeds normally |
| **Vertical exit** | Alliance sells or IPOs a complete vertical (the entire fintech vertical, or the entire RWA vertical) to a strategic acquirer or to the public market | Remaining verticals continue under the Alliance |
| **Coordination-layer exit** | The Alliance's parent holding takes a public listing; substrate, IP, brand registry, and member-relationship network become public-market assets under a single ticker | Member-level operations continue under each member's existing structure |

The three levels are non-mutually-exclusive. A typical multi-year
trajectory contemplates several individual-member exits before any
vertical exit, with the coordination-layer public listing reserved for
year five and beyond.

## The PayPal-Mafia analogy — and where it ends

The Alliance is deliberately analogous to the PayPal alumni network in its
outcome: a coordinated cohort of people and companies in which one
member's win seeds the next, distribution flows freely across members,
capital recycles into new ventures, and the network as a whole produces
more value than the sum of its parts.

The analogy ends in one important way. The PayPal alumni network was
**discovered ex post**, after the PayPal company itself exited. Its
coordination was informal — friends helping friends, with no contracted
network economics. The Alliance is the opposite: it is **engineered ex
ante**, with contracted IP licensing, contracted distribution sharing,
contracted capital flows, and contracted governance. The compounding is
guaranteed by the structure rather than left to social capital alone.
